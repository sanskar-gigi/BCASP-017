Elbert Hubbard was an American writer, publisher, and philosopher who lived from 1856 to 1915. He is best known for his inspirational essays and his role in the Arts and Crafts Movement of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Hubbard was born in Bloomington, Illinois, and grew up on a farm. He was largely self-educated, but he developed a love for literature and began writing at a young age. He worked as a traveling salesman for a soap company before founding his own publishing company, the Roycroft Press, in East Aurora, New York. Hubbard's writing was known for its straightforward, no-nonsense style and its focus on self-improvement and personal responsibility. He wrote about a wide range of topics, including art, business, religion, and politics. His most famous work is probably "A Message to Garcia," a short essay about the importance of taking initiative and getting things done. In addition to his writing, Hubbard was an active member of the Arts and Crafts Movement, which sought to promote traditional craftsmanship and reject mass production. He designed furniture and decorative objects, and he was a strong advocate for the value of manual labor. Hubbard's life was tragically cut short when he died in the sinking of the RMS Lusitania in 1915. However, his legacy lives on through his writing and his contributions to the Arts and Crafts Movement. He remains an inspiration to many who seek to live a life of purpose and meaning.
